# Causal Structure: Carbon Fixation Collapse and Global Warming

*A node-based map of the causal relationships between CO₂ emissions, carbon fixation system collapse, natural circulation breakdown, and global warming acceleration.*

---

## Overview

This document provides a structured, node-based breakdown of the causal model introduced in the README of this repository.

The central claim is that global warming must be understood through two parallel causal pathways:

1. **The emission pathway** — human activity increases atmospheric CO₂ directly through burning and land use.
2. **The fixation collapse pathway** — human activity degrades the natural systems that absorb, fix, store, and recycle carbon, reducing Earth's capacity to process the carbon already in circulation.

Both pathways increase atmospheric CO₂. Both contribute to warming. Current mainstream climate communication emphasizes the first; this repository argues the second is equally important and structurally underemphasized.

---

## Model Comparison

### Model A: Single-Cause Linear Model (Common Public Understanding)

```
Human activity
→ Increased CO₂ emissions
→ CO₂ accumulates in atmosphere
→ Greenhouse effect intensifies
→ Earth surface temperature rises
→ Global warming

Implied solution:
→ Reduce CO₂ emissions
```

**What this model captures:** The physical greenhouse mechanism. Scientifically valid at the atmospheric level. Forms the basis of mainstream climate policy.

**What this model omits:** The sink side of the carbon cycle. The degradation of carbon-processing systems. Feedback loops between warming and sink collapse. The fact that CO₂ accumulation reflects not only excess input but also reduced processing capacity.

---

### Model B: Multi-Cause System Model (This Repository's Framework)

```
Human activity
→ [Emission pathway]     CO₂ emissions increase directly
→ [Fixation pathway]     Soils, forests, oceans, microbes, wetlands degrade
                         → Carbon fixation capacity declines
                         → Carbon absorption capacity declines
                         → CO₂ retention in atmosphere increases

Both pathways
→ Atmospheric CO₂ concentration rises
→ Greenhouse forcing increases
→ Surface temperature rises (global warming)
→ Thermal inertia delays full effect
→ Warming feeds back into further fixation collapse
→ Feedback loop amplifies warming
```

**What this model adds:** The sink-side dynamics. The biological, soil, and oceanic infrastructure that determines how much CO₂ the planet can absorb. Positive feedback loops between warming and fixation decline. Additional intervention points beyond emission reduction.

## Node Catalog

### Emission Nodes

| Node ID | Node Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| E-1 | Fossil Fuel Combustion | CO₂ from coal, oil, gas combustion for energy and transport |
| E-2 | Deforestation and Burning | CO₂ released when forests are cleared and burned |
| E-3 | Agricultural Land Use | CH₄ from livestock, N₂O from synthetic fertilizers, soil carbon loss from tillage |
| E-4 | Industrial Processes | Cement, steel, chemical manufacturing emissions |
| E-5 | Organic Waste Incineration | CO₂ and CH₄ from burning food waste, crop residue, and organic material |
| E-6 | Land-Use Change | Release of carbon stored in native soils and biomass during land conversion |

---

### Atmospheric Nodes

| Node ID | Node Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| A-1 | Atmospheric CO₂ Concentration | The net balance between total emissions and total absorption; the primary greenhouse forcing variable |
| A-2 | Greenhouse Radiative Forcing | The net change in Earth's energy balance due to elevated CO₂ and other greenhouse gases |
| A-3 | Global Mean Surface Temperature | The aggregate surface warming response to radiative forcing |
| A-4 | Thermal Inertia | The time delay between greenhouse forcing changes and full temperature response, primarily due to ocean heat buffering |
| A-5 | Regional Climate Instability | Increased frequency and intensity of drought, flood, heat waves, and ecosystem disruption |

*Note on Thermal Inertia (A-4):* Even if atmospheric CO₂ were stabilized today, committed warming from the existing greenhouse forcing would continue for decades. This means that damage to carbon fixation systems has long-term effects that extend beyond the period of active degradation.

---

### Carbon Fixation Nodes

Carbon fixation refers to the biological and geochemical conversion of atmospheric or dissolved CO₂ into organic or mineral carbon forms that are stored outside the atmosphere.

| Node ID | Node Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| F-1 | Forest Photosynthetic Fixation | CO₂ absorbed and stored as biomass in trees, understory, and root systems |
| F-2 | Soil Organic Carbon | Carbon stored in decomposed organic matter, humus, and mineral-associated organic compounds in soil |
| F-3 | Soil Microbial Carbon Processing | Microbial conversion of organic matter into stable soil carbon; microbial necromass as long-term carbon pool |
| F-4 | Mycorrhizal and Root Networks | Fungal-plant symbiosis enabling deep soil carbon transfer and stabilization |
| F-5 | Wetland and Peatland Carbon | Long-term carbon accumulation in water-saturated, low-decomposition environments |
| F-6 | Phytoplankton Primary Production | Photosynthetic fixation of dissolved CO₂ in the ocean surface layer |
| F-7 | Marine Biological Carbon Pump | Downward transport of organic carbon from ocean surface to deep layers via sinking biomass and fecal material |
| F-8 | Coastal Ecosystem Carbon | Carbon fixed and stored in seagrass beds, mangroves, kelp forests, and salt marshes (blue carbon) |
| F-9 | Grassland and Savanna Carbon | Carbon stored in perennial grass root systems and associated soils |

---

### Carbon Absorption Nodes

Carbon absorption refers to the net uptake of CO₂ from the atmosphere by surface systems, distinct from fixation which emphasizes the conversion and stabilization process.

| Node ID | Node Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| B-1 | Terrestrial Net Biome Production | Net carbon balance of all land ecosystems; positive = net sink, negative = net source |
| B-2 | Ocean Surface CO₂ Uptake | Physical dissolution of atmospheric CO₂ into surface seawater, modulated by temperature and carbonate chemistry |
| B-3 | Airborne Fraction | Proportion of annual CO₂ emissions remaining in the atmosphere after land and ocean uptake; indicator of combined sink strength |

---

### Degradation Pressure Nodes

| Node ID | Node Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| P-1 | Deforestation Rate | Annual loss of forest cover; direct destruction of fixation infrastructure |
| P-2 | Soil Degradation | Loss of topsoil, humus, and microbial diversity through industrial tillage, compaction, and monoculture |
| P-3 | Synthetic Chemical Inputs | Pesticide and herbicide effects on soil microbiome; synthetic fertilizers suppressing natural nitrogen and carbon cycling |
| P-4 | Wetland and Peatland Drainage | Conversion of high-carbon-density ecosystems to agricultural or urban land; releases stored carbon |
| P-5 | Ocean Pollution and Acidification | Plastic pollution, agricultural runoff, and CO₂-driven acidification stressing phytoplankton and marine fixation systems |
| P-6 | Organic Matter Removal | Removal of crop residue, leaf litter, and food waste from natural cycling; reduces humus formation inputs |
| P-7 | Biodiversity Loss | Reduction of species diversity weakens ecosystem resilience, pollination, nutrient cycling, and carbon stabilization |

## Causal Pathways

### Pathway 1: Direct Emission → Atmospheric CO₂ Rise

```
E-1, E-2, E-3, E-4, E-5, E-6
             ↓
            A-1 ↑  (atmospheric CO₂ rises directly)
             ↓
            A-2 ↑  (radiative forcing increases)
             ↓
            A-3 ↑  (surface temperature rises)
       [modulated by A-4: thermal inertia]
```

---

### Pathway 2: Fixation Collapse → Reduced CO₂ Processing → Atmospheric CO₂ Rise

```
P-1 → F-1 ↓   (deforestation reduces forest fixation)
P-2 → F-2 ↓, F-3 ↓   (soil degradation reduces soil carbon and microbial processing)
P-3 → F-3 ↓, F-4 ↓   (chemical inputs suppress microbial and mycorrhizal systems)
P-4 → F-5 ↓   (wetland drainage destroys long-term carbon storage)
P-5 → F-6 ↓, F-7 ↓   (ocean stress reduces phytoplankton and biological pump)
P-6 → F-2 ↓, F-3 ↓   (organic matter removal reduces humus formation inputs)
P-7 → F-1 ↓, F-3 ↓, F-6 ↓ (biodiversity loss weakens all fixation systems)

All fixation declines
→ B-1 ↓, B-2 ↓  (net absorption by land and ocean weakens)
→ B-3 ↑  (airborne fraction rises: more emitted CO₂ stays in atmosphere)
→ A-1 ↑  (atmospheric CO₂ rises even without emission growth)
→ A-2 ↑ → A-3 ↑  (warming)
```

---

### Pathway 3: Warming Feeds Back into Fixation Collapse

```
A-3 ↑, A-5 ↑ (warming and climate instability)
→ Heat stress, drought on forests → F-1 ↓
→ Soil drying and temperature rise → F-3 acceleration (faster decomposition, less stabilization) → F-2 ↓
→ Ocean surface warming and stratification → F-6 ↓, F-7 ↓
→ Permafrost thaw → F-5 ↓ + large stored carbon release

These declines loop back to A-1 ↑ → A-3 ↑ (amplifying feedback)
```

## Positive Feedback Loops

Positive feedback loops are self-amplifying dynamics where warming worsens the conditions that cause further warming.

---

### Loop 1: Warming → Forest Stress → Fixation Loss → More Warming

```
A-3 ↑
→ drought, heat waves (A-5 ↑)
→ forest dieback, reduced photosynthesis (F-1 ↓)
→ B-1 ↓ (terrestrial sink weakens or reverses)
→ A-1 ↑
→ A-2 ↑ → A-3 ↑   [loop closes]
```

**Evidence:** Amazon dieback documented (Gatti et al. 2021, *Science*). Boreal forest fire events increasing. Mediterranean and Western US forest mortality from drought.

---

### Loop 2: Warming → Soil Respiration Acceleration → Soil Carbon Loss → More Warming

```
A-3 ↑
→ soil temperature rises
→ F-3 microbial decomposition accelerates (carbon release exceeds input)
→ F-2 ↓ (net soil carbon stock declines)
→ A-1 ↑ → A-3 ↑   [loop closes]
```

**Evidence:** Crowther et al. (2016, *Nature*) demonstrated significant soil carbon loss under warming. Permafrost thaw in Arctic releasing stored carbon.

---

### Loop 3: Warming → Ocean Stratification → Phytoplankton Decline → Reduced Marine Sink

```
A-3 ↑, A-4 ↑ (ocean heat content rises)
→ surface ocean warms and stratifies
→ reduced upwelling of nutrient-rich deep water
→ F-6 ↓ (phytoplankton nutrient-limited)
→ F-7 ↓ (biological carbon pump weakens)
→ B-2 ↓ (ocean CO₂ uptake declines)
→ A-1 ↑ → A-3 ↑   [loop closes]
```

**Evidence:** Phytoplankton biomass decline reported in subtropical gyres. Ocean stratification increasing under warming. Net ocean carbon uptake efficiency declining in some models.

---

### Loop 4: Degraded Carbon Fixation → Reduced Ecosystem Resilience → Accelerated Degradation

```
P-1 through P-7 pressures
→ F-1 through F-9 fixation declines
→ B-1 ↓ (ecosystem weakens)
→ A-5 ↑ (more climate instability: drought, fire, flood)
→ further P-1, P-7 degradation (fires destroy forests; drought kills microbes)
→ F-1 ↓ further   [loop closes without warming as intermediate]
```

This loop can self-amplify independently of direct temperature effects once ecosystem resilience falls below critical thresholds.

---

### Stabilizing Mechanism (Natural; Currently Weakening)

```
A-1 ↑ (elevated CO₂)
→ CO₂ fertilization effect on some plants (partial F-1 increase in some regions)
→ B-1 partial positive offset
→ A-1 modest decrease
```

**Caveat:** The CO₂ fertilization effect is strongly limited by water, nutrient, and temperature constraints. Under drought and soil degradation conditions, this stabilizing effect is significantly reduced or negated. It is not sufficient to offset net fixation decline at current warming and degradation rates.

---

## Summary: The Full Causal Equation

From a systems perspective, atmospheric CO₂ growth can be expressed as:

```
ΔAtmospheric CO₂ =
  + Total CO₂ emissions (E-1 through E-6)
  − Terrestrial net carbon uptake (B-1)
  − Ocean net carbon uptake (B-2)
  + Carbon released by ecosystem degradation (from F-1 through F-9 decline)
  + Permafrost and wetland carbon release (F-5 collapse)
```

When the degradation terms increase and the uptake terms decrease simultaneously with rising emissions, CO₂ accumulates faster than emission rates alone would suggest.

This is the structural explanation for why atmospheric CO₂ continues to rise despite growing awareness of the problem.

## Limitations of This Document

1. Causal arrows represent hypothesized or empirically supported relationships. Quantitative magnitudes of individual node contributions are not specified. Refer to HYPOTHESES_AND_VERIFICATION.md for evidence status of each claim.

2. Timescales are not specified. Different nodes operate on different timescales (seasonal, decadal, millennial). This document treats all nodes synchronically for structural clarity.

3. Regional variability is not captured. Carbon fixation dynamics vary significantly across biomes and ocean regions. This document presents a globally aggregated model.

4. The node boundaries are not perfectly discrete. Many nodes interact bidirectionally and nonlinearly.
