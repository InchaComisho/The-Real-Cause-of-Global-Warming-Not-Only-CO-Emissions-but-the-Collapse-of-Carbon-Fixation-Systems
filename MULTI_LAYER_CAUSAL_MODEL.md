# Multi-Layer Causal Model of Global Warming

## A Conceptual Eight-Layer Framework from Industrial Activity to Climate Feedback

---

> **Scope and Validation Note**
>
> This document presents a conceptual multi-layer causal model. It is intended as a structural thinking framework, not as a predictive model or policy prescription. All layers and their interactions are conceptual and require scientific validation. This framework does not deny the greenhouse effect of CO₂ or the importance of emissions reduction. It proposes that the full causal chain extends deeper than emissions alone.

---

## Overview

Most public explanations of global warming present a two- or three-layer causal chain:

```text
Industrial activity
→ CO₂ emissions
→ Global warming
```

This is scientifically valid as far as it goes.

This document proposes a more complete eight-layer causal model, tracing the full chain from human activity through ecological degradation, carbon sink collapse, and climate feedback loops.

---

## The Eight-Layer Causal Model

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Layer 1: Industrial CO₂ Emissions                              │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 2: Land-Use Change and Deforestation                     │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 3: Soil Degradation and Microbial Decline                │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 4: Ocean Circulation Weakening and Plankton Decline      │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 5: Carbon Sink Capacity Reduction                        │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 6: Atmospheric CO₂ Accumulation                          │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 7: Thermal Imbalance and Climate Destabilization         │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Layer 8: Feedback Loops — Drought, Wildfire, Desertification,  │
│           and Ecosystem Collapse                                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Each layer feeds into the next. Layers 7 and 8 also feed back into Layers 3–5, creating a self-amplifying cycle.

---

## Layer 1: Industrial CO₂ Emissions

**Primary drivers:**
- Fossil fuel combustion (coal, oil, natural gas)
- Industrial manufacturing processes
- Transportation and aviation
- Energy production systems

**What this layer explains:**
The direct input of CO₂ and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere from human industrial activity.

This is the most widely acknowledged layer.

It is necessary to address, but it represents only part of the causal structure.

---

## Layer 2: Land-Use Change and Deforestation

**Primary drivers:**
- Conversion of forests to agricultural land
- Expansion of monoculture cropping systems
- Urbanization and land sealing
- Infrastructure development
- Overgrazing and pasture degradation

**What this layer explains:**
When forests are cleared, two things happen simultaneously:

1. Stored carbon in biomass is released as CO₂ through burning or decomposition.
2. A functioning carbon sink is removed from the system.

This creates both an emissions source and a sink deficit.

Land-use change is estimated to contribute significantly to total anthropogenic CO₂ emissions. Beyond emissions, deforestation also disrupts water cycles, soil formation, and ecological continuity.

---

## Layer 3: Soil Degradation and Microbial Decline

**Primary drivers:**
- Overuse of synthetic chemical fertilizers and pesticides
- Excessive tillage and soil compaction
- Reduction in organic matter return to soil
- Loss of crop and plant diversity
- Wetland drainage and peatland disturbance

**What this layer explains:**
Soils contain a vast reservoir of organic carbon. The FAO and UNFCCC report that global soils hold approximately twice the carbon stored in the atmosphere.

When soils degrade:
- Soil organic carbon is released as CO₂
- Microbial communities that process and stabilize carbon are depleted
- Humus formation slows or reverses
- Water retention capacity declines, reducing drought resilience
- The soil's function as a long-term carbon store is diminished

Soil microbial decline is particularly significant. Soil microorganisms are carbon processors: they transform organic matter into stable soil carbon compounds. When microbial ecosystems collapse, the biological machinery of carbon storage is weakened.

---

## Layer 4: Ocean Circulation Weakening and Plankton Decline

**Primary drivers:**
- Rising sea surface temperatures increasing thermal stratification
- Weakening of thermohaline circulation
- Ocean acidification from CO₂ absorption
- Runoff of synthetic chemicals and nutrients
- Microplastic accumulation
- Dead zone expansion from nutrient imbalances

**What this layer explains:**
Oceans absorb roughly 25–30% of annual anthropogenic CO₂ emissions.

This ocean carbon sink depends on:
- Healthy phytoplankton populations that fix carbon through photosynthesis
- Functioning biological carbon pumps that transfer surface carbon to deep water
- Vertical circulation that brings nutrients from depth to surface waters

When oceans warm and stratify, vertical mixing is reduced. Nutrient upwelling declines. Phytoplankton productivity falls. The biological carbon pump weakens.

At the same time, ocean acidification alters the chemistry of carbonate systems, potentially reducing the ocean's capacity to buffer CO₂ absorption.

---

## Layer 5: Carbon Sink Capacity Reduction

**What this layer explains:**
Layers 2, 3, and 4 collectively reduce the Earth's total carbon sink capacity.

The global carbon sink efficiency — the proportion of emitted CO₂ that the land and ocean can absorb — may be declining as ecosystems weaken.

When carbon sink capacity is reduced:
- A larger fraction of emitted CO₂ remains in the atmosphere
- Previously stable carbon stored in soils, forests, and permafrost may be released
- The time for atmospheric CO₂ concentration to stabilize after emissions reduction is extended

This is the critical transition point: the climate system moves from a state where natural sinks can partially offset emissions to a state where they cannot.

---

## Layer 6: Atmospheric CO₂ Accumulation

**What this layer explains:**
The result of the combination of:
- High ongoing emissions (Layer 1)
- Additional emissions from land-use change (Layer 2)
- Carbon released from degraded soils and ecosystems (Layers 3–4)
- Reduced sink capacity (Layer 5)

Atmospheric CO₂ concentration rises faster than would occur from emissions alone.

This layer is where the standard public explanation begins: elevated CO₂ enhances the greenhouse effect and contributes to warming. That explanation is valid — but it is the midpoint of the causal chain, not the beginning.

---

## Layer 7: Thermal Imbalance and Climate Destabilization

**What this layer explains:**
Rising atmospheric CO₂ reduces outgoing infrared radiation, increasing net radiative forcing.

The Earth system accumulates heat.

Effects include:
- Rising global mean surface temperature
- Ocean heat content increase
- Ice sheet and glacier retreat
- Sea level rise
- Shift in precipitation patterns
- Increased frequency and intensity of extreme weather events
- Ocean stratification acceleration

---

## Layer 8: Feedback Loops — Drought, Wildfire, Desertification, and Ecosystem Collapse

**What this layer explains:**
Thermal imbalance and climate destabilization (Layer 7) create conditions that further damage the carbon fixation systems identified in Layers 2–5.

This is the feedback connection that closes the loop:

```text
Drought
→ forest stress, tree mortality, reduced photosynthesis
→ weakened terrestrial carbon sink (Layer 3 degradation)

Wildfire
→ rapid release of stored carbon from forests and soils
→ direct CO₂ emissions + carbon sink loss

Desertification
→ loss of vegetated land, soil carbon, and microbial systems
→ reduced land carbon fixation capacity (Layers 3 and 5)

Ecosystem collapse
→ biodiversity loss, food web disruption
→ reduced ecological resilience
→ impaired recovery of carbon fixation systems
```

Each of these feedback pathways connects Layer 8 back to Layers 3–5, creating self-amplifying cycles that can accelerate CO₂ accumulation and warming independently of human emission levels.


## Why This Model Matters for Climate Strategy

If global warming is understood only through Layers 1 and 6–7, the policy space is narrow:

```text
Reduce emissions → reduce CO₂ → reduce warming
```

If global warming is understood through all eight layers, the policy space expands:

```text
Reduce emissions (Layer 1)
+ Halt deforestation and land degradation (Layer 2)
+ Restore soil health and microbial systems (Layer 3)
+ Support ocean circulation and plankton recovery (Layer 4)
+ Strengthen carbon sink capacity (Layer 5)
= More effective and durable climate stabilization
```

This does not mean emissions reduction is less important.

It means emissions reduction alone may be insufficient if Layers 2–5 continue to degrade.
