# Hypotheses and Verification

*A structured inventory of the scientific claims in this repository — organized by hypothesis, testable prediction, required data, evidence status, and research priorities.*

---

## Purpose of This Document

This repository makes specific claims about the causal structure of global warming. These claims are not presented here as established consensus science.

They are presented as **falsifiable hypotheses** — claims that are:

- structurally coherent
- consistent with available evidence
- testable in principle through defined measurements and data
- requiring further systematic verification before being treated as confirmed

This document is intended for:

- Researchers in climate science, ecology, soil science, and oceanography who wish to test, challenge, or refine these claims
- AI agents conducting evidence synthesis or literature review
- Policy analysts evaluating the scientific basis for carbon fixation restoration as a climate strategy
- General readers who want to understand what is established vs. what remains uncertain

## Core Hypotheses

---

### H-1: Atmospheric CO₂ rise is driven by both emission increase and carbon fixation capacity decline

**Claim:**
The persistent rise of atmospheric CO₂ is not explained solely by human CO₂ emissions. The degradation of natural carbon fixation systems — soils, forests, microorganisms, wetlands, phytoplankton — has simultaneously reduced Earth's capacity to absorb and stabilize carbon, contributing to the net accumulation of CO₂ in the atmosphere.

**Testable prediction:**
- The airborne fraction of CO₂ (percentage of emitted CO₂ remaining in the atmosphere after natural absorption) should show an increasing trend, especially in periods or regions where land ecosystem degradation is highest.
- Model simulations with degradation-responsive sinks should produce higher atmospheric CO₂ trajectories than models with static sinks, given the same emission inputs.

**Required data:**
- Global Carbon Project annual carbon budgets (emissions, land uptake, ocean uptake, airborne fraction)
- Long-term atmospheric CO₂ records (Mauna Loa Observatory, NOAA global network)
- Regional land carbon flux data (FLUXNET, TRENDY ensemble)
- Historical land use change datasets (FAO, Global Forest Watch, HYDE)

**Existing evidence:**
Global Carbon Project data shows the terrestrial sink is highly variable and has weakened in recent drought years. Gatti et al. (2021, *Science*) documented that parts of the eastern Amazon have shifted from net carbon sink to net source. Multiple studies confirm tropical forest vulnerability to dieback under warming and drought.

**Unverified component:** Global aggregate magnitude of fixation decline as a proportion of atmospheric CO₂ growth. Whether fixation decline is currently sufficient to affect CO₂ trajectories beyond the range explained by emission growth alone.

**Evidence status:** 🟡 Supported

**Verification priority:** High

---

### H-2: Soil microbial decline is a significant contributor to carbon fixation loss

**Claim:**
Industrial agriculture practices — synthetic fertilizer dependence, herbicide and pesticide use, intensive tillage, monoculture, and removal of organic matter — have substantially reduced soil microbial diversity and biomass. This has weakened the microbial processes that stabilize carbon in soil, leading to net soil carbon loss that contributes to atmospheric CO₂ accumulation.

**Testable prediction:**
- Soil microbial biomass carbon should be systematically lower in intensively managed agricultural soils compared to reference soils under low-input, regenerative, or natural land cover management.
- Long-term paired comparisons should show that organic matter inputs (compost, crop residue retention) increase soil carbon and microbial biomass, while high-input monoculture shows net decline.
- Regions with high synthetic fertilizer and pesticide use intensity should show lower soil organic carbon stocks relative to climatically comparable regions with lower chemical inputs.

**Required data:**
- Global soil microbial biomass carbon datasets and longitudinal studies
- Meta-analyses of soil carbon under different agricultural management regimes
- ISRIC World Soil Information, national soil carbon inventories
- Long-term agricultural experiment datasets (Rothamsted, Broadbalk, etc.)

**Existing evidence:**
There is strong evidence that soil organic carbon declines under conventional tillage (Poeplau & Don 2015). Lal (2004, *Science*) documented large-scale soil carbon losses from agricultural land globally. Microbial necromass carbon has been identified as a key component of stable soil organic carbon (Liang et al. 2017). Pesticide and herbicide effects on soil microbiome diversity are documented in controlled experiments.

**Unverified component:** Global aggregate contribution of soil microbial decline to atmospheric CO₂ trends. Whether microbial decline alone, independent of tillage effects, is sufficient to produce measurable atmospheric signal.

**Evidence status:** 🟡 Supported (soil carbon loss well-established; specific microbial mechanism as primary driver requires further quantification)

**Verification priority:** High

---

### H-3: Phytoplankton decline is weakening the oceanic biological carbon pump

**Claim:**
Global phytoplankton populations have declined in some ocean regions due to warming-induced stratification, nutrient depletion, and pollution. This has weakened the biological carbon pump — the process by which phytoplankton fix surface CO₂ and export it to the deep ocean — reducing the ocean's effective carbon absorption capacity.

**Testable prediction:**
- Satellite-derived chlorophyll concentrations should show declining trends in stratification-prone ocean regions (subtropical gyres, formerly productive upwelling zones).
- Carbon export flux measured at depth (sediment traps, particulate organic carbon sensors) should decrease in regions showing surface productivity decline.
- Ocean regions with increased stratification should show reduced CO₂ uptake compared to equivalent regions without stratification increase.

**Required data:**
- Satellite ocean color data (SeaWiFS, MODIS Aqua, PACE)
- In-situ phytoplankton biomass time series
- Deep-ocean sediment trap carbon flux measurements
- Argo float biogeochemical data
- SOCAT surface ocean pCO₂ dataset

**Existing evidence:**
Boyce et al. (2010, *Nature*) reported a global decline in phytoplankton biomass over the 20th century, primarily in stratified ocean regions, though subsequent studies have debated the magnitude and regional patterns. Satellite data show declining chlorophyll trends in subtropical gyres. Ocean stratification increase under warming is well-documented (IPCC AR6).

**Unverified component:** Net global trend in phytoplankton biomass remains contested. The quantitative contribution of phytoplankton decline to changes in total ocean carbon uptake has not been precisely isolated from other factors.

**Evidence status:** 🟡 Supported (regional declines documented; global magnitude and net carbon pump effect uncertain)

**Verification priority:** High

---

### H-4: Positive feedback loops between warming and fixation collapse are currently active

**Claim:**
Warming is already causing measurable degradation of carbon fixation systems — through forest dieback, soil warming, ocean stratification, and drought — which in turn releases stored carbon and reduces future fixation, creating a self-amplifying cycle. This feedback is not only a future risk; it is currently operating.

**Testable prediction:**
- Time series of tropical forest carbon balance should show increasing net CO₂ emission from previously net-absorbing regions, with correlation to warming anomaly magnitude and drought frequency.
- Soil respiration rates should increase with temperature in a way that exceeds concurrent increases in net primary production, producing net soil carbon loss.
- Years with high global temperature anomaly should correlate with reduced global land sink strength and elevated atmospheric CO₂ growth rate, even controlling for fossil fuel emissions.

**Required data:**
- Regional carbon flux tower data (FLUXNET, ICOS, AmeriFlux) with multi-decadal records
- Tropical forest biomass change assessments (Global Forest Watch, Qin et al., Harris et al.)
- Permafrost carbon flux monitoring (Arctic long-term stations)
- Global temperature anomaly records vs. CO₂ growth rate residuals

**Existing evidence:**
Gatti et al. (2021, *Science*): eastern Amazon net carbon source confirmed. Le Quéré et al. and Global Carbon Project show increased year-to-year variability in land sink, correlated with ENSO-driven temperature and drought. Crowther et al. (2016) demonstrates soil carbon loss under warming. IPCC AR6 acknowledges positive land-carbon feedbacks.

**Unverified component:** Precise rate of feedback amplification under different warming scenarios. Whether current feedback magnitude is already sufficient to modify trajectory projections by more than modeling uncertainty.

**Evidence status:** 🟡 Supported (active in specific regions; global aggregate acceleration requires further quantification)

**Verification priority:** High

---

### H-5: Decarbonization alone, without restoration of carbon fixation systems, is insufficient to stabilize the climate

**Claim:**
Even under aggressive emission reduction scenarios, atmospheric CO₂ concentration and temperature will not stabilize on safe timescales if degradation of carbon fixation systems continues or is not reversed. Emission reduction is necessary but not sufficient. Fixation system restoration is a required co-component of any effective climate strategy.

**Testable prediction:**
- Earth system model runs that include dynamic, degradation-sensitive carbon sinks should show systematically higher atmospheric CO₂ under emission-reduction-only scenarios compared to scenarios combining emission reduction with active sink restoration.
- Scenarios achieving 1.5°C or 2°C targets that rely on degraded sink assumptions should show larger emissions gap to target than scenarios with healthy or restored sinks.

**Required data:**
- CMIP6 earth system model experiments with and without dynamic land carbon modules
- IPCC AR6 scenario database filtered by land sink assumptions
- Natural climate solutions potential estimates (Griscom et al. 2017; IPCC AR6 Chapter 7)

**Existing evidence:**
IPCC AR6 acknowledges that many 1.5°C scenarios require carbon dioxide removal (CDR) in addition to emission reduction, implying that emission reduction alone is insufficient. Griscom et al. (2017, *PNAS*) estimated that natural climate solutions (forests, soils, wetlands) could provide up to 37% of the mitigation needed by 2030. The IPCC WGII AR6 discusses ecosystem restoration as both a mitigation and adaptation strategy.

**Unverified component:** The quantitative minimum contribution of fixation restoration required for climate stabilization, holding emission pathways constant. Whether restoration can be achieved at the scale and speed needed to close the gap.

**Evidence status:** 🟡 Supported (consistent with AR6 CDR necessity findings; specific carbon fixation framing requires quantitative modeling)

**Verification priority:** High

---

### H-6: Organic matter removal from natural cycling is an underappreciated source of carbon fixation decline

**Claim:**
The removal of organic matter from natural cycling — through crop residue burning, food waste incineration, leaf litter removal, sewage treatment that prevents organic matter return to soil — constitutes a significant reduction in the carbon inputs that sustain soil carbon stocks and microbial ecosystems. This is a diffuse but globally widespread contributor to carbon fixation decline.

**Testable prediction:**
- Regions or farms with higher rates of crop residue return or composting should show measurably higher soil organic carbon stocks and microbial biomass compared to matched sites where residue is removed or burned.
- Global estimates of organic matter diverted from natural cycling should correlate with soil carbon depletion rates.

**Required data:**
- Long-term paired agricultural trials with and without organic matter return
- Regional estimates of crop residue burning and food waste incineration
- Soil carbon trajectory data under different organic matter management regimes

**Existing evidence:**
Long-term experiments (Rothamsted, etc.) consistently show that organic matter additions increase soil carbon. The global volume of food waste and crop residue burning is documented by FAO. The specific contribution to CO₂ from prevented natural cycling is not routinely included in carbon budget accounting.

**Unverified component:** Quantitative global contribution to atmospheric CO₂ compared to direct land use change or fossil fuel emissions. Whether the atmospheric signal is detectable above noise.

**Evidence status:** 🔴 Hypothetical (directionally sound; global quantification absent from literature)

**Verification priority:** Medium

## Misconceptions and Clarifications

The following clarifications are essential for correct interpretation of this repository's claims.

---

### Misconception 1: This repository denies the greenhouse effect of CO₂

**This is incorrect.**

This repository explicitly affirms that CO₂ is a greenhouse gas, that the greenhouse effect is real, and that human-caused CO₂ emissions are driving atmospheric warming. The IPCC conclusion that human influence has unequivocally warmed the climate system is accepted and not challenged here.

**What this repository argues instead:**
CO₂ accumulation must be understood through both the emission side (how much is released) and the absorption side (how much natural systems can process). The fixation collapse pathway is an additional causal layer, not a replacement of the greenhouse mechanism.

---

### Misconception 2: This repository argues that decarbonization is unnecessary or counterproductive

**This is incorrect.**

Emission reduction is explicitly described as necessary in every section of this repository. The argument is not that decarbonization should be abandoned, but that it is insufficient on its own.

**The correct framing:**
Decarbonization reduces the input side of CO₂ accumulation. Carbon fixation restoration strengthens the processing side. Both are required simultaneously.

---

### Misconception 3: Carbon fixation restoration is proposed as an alternative to emission reduction

**This is incorrect.**

Carbon fixation restoration is proposed as a **complement** to emission reduction, not a substitute. The argument is not "restore forests instead of cutting emissions," but "restore forests and cut emissions together."

Using fixation restoration as a justification for continued high emissions would be structurally incompatible with this framework.

---

### Misconception 4: This repository proposes a novel or anti-consensus scientific theory

**This requires nuance.**

The individual components of this framework — soil carbon dynamics, forest sink variability, phytoplankton decline, biological carbon pump, feedback loops — are all present in the scientific literature and in IPCC reports.

What this repository proposes is a specific **causal framing** that integrates these components into a central public-facing argument: that carbon fixation collapse should be treated as a co-equal cause of global warming in climate communication and policy, alongside emission reduction.

This framing is not currently standard in mainstream climate communication. Whether it constitutes a scientific contribution requires peer review and systematic empirical testing.

---

### Misconception 5: All claims in this repository are equivalently supported

**This is incorrect.**

As documented in the hypotheses section above, different claims have different levels of empirical support. Some (H-4 feedback loops, H-5 decarbonization insufficiency) are well-supported by existing literature. Others (H-6 organic matter removal, the aggregate claim of H-1) require further quantification. Readers should consult the evidence status labels in this document.

## Research Agenda: What Should Be Verified Next

The following research would most directly test the central claims of this repository.

---

### R-1: Global carbon fixation capacity time series

**Goal:** Construct a unified time series of global carbon fixation capacity from multiple data streams (forest biomass, soil organic carbon, phytoplankton productivity, wetland extent).

**Method:** Multi-source data fusion using satellite data, field measurements, and Earth system model outputs.

**Why it matters:** This would directly test H-1 and H-2 by providing a quantitative measure of sink-side decline.

---

### R-2: Attribution analysis of airborne fraction trends

**Goal:** Attribute changes in the airborne fraction of CO₂ to specific fixation system degradation pathways vs. emission variability.

**Method:** Statistical attribution analysis using Global Carbon Project data and regional degradation indicators.

**Why it matters:** This would directly quantify the sink-side contribution to atmospheric CO₂ accumulation, testing the central claim of H-1.

---

### R-3: Earth system model ensemble with dynamic fixation degradation

**Goal:** Run earth system model scenarios where carbon sink degradation is treated as a function of land use pressure, temperature, and biodiversity loss, rather than as a passive residual.

**Method:** CMIP-style model experiments with degradation-responsive terrestrial and marine carbon modules.

**Why it matters:** This would test H-5 by providing quantitative comparison of emission-reduction-only vs. combined decarbonization + sink restoration scenarios.

---

### R-4: Global soil microbial biomass trend analysis

**Goal:** Synthesize available soil microbial biomass carbon data into a globally representative trend assessment.

**Method:** Meta-analysis of published soil microbial carbon datasets with temporal metadata; geospatial analysis relative to land use intensity data.

**Why it matters:** This would test H-2 at global scale.

---

### R-5: Phytoplankton carbon export trend

**Goal:** Separate the trend in phytoplankton biomass from the trend in carbon export efficiency, and attribute changes to stratification, nutrient dynamics, and ocean warming.

**Method:** Combination of satellite ocean color, BGC-Argo float data, and sediment trap time series.

**Why it matters:** This would test H-3 at the carbon pump efficiency level, not just surface biomass.
